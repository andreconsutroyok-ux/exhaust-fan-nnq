Exhaust Fan

1.  What's an Exhaust Fan?

Basically, it's a fan that sucks out bad air (like stinky smells, smoke, moisture, or just plain hot air) from a room and brings in fresh air. It keeps things from getting too stuffy.

2.  What are they good for?

*   Fresh Air: Gets rid of old air and brings in the good stuff.
*   Smell Control: Gets rid of cooking smells, smoke, and chemical odors.
*   Dries things out: Cuts down on moisture in kitchens, bathrooms, and laundry rooms.
*   Cooling: Helps kick out hot air from rooms, attics, or shops.
*   Keeps you healthy: Reduces dust, allergens, and bad gases floating around.

3.  Types of Exhaust Fans

A. Where you put them:

*   Wall Fans:

    *   Go on outside walls.
    *   Blow air straight outside.
    *   Seen a lot in bathrooms and kitchens.
*   Ceiling Fans:

    *   Go in the ceiling.
    *   Great for bathrooms, kitchens, and shops.
    *   Can have pipes to send air outside.
*   Window Fans:

    *   They fit right in a window.
    *   Good if you don't have outside walls.

B. What they're used for:

*   Bathroom Fans:

    *   Small and can handle moisture.
    *   Stop mold from growing.
*   Kitchen Fans:

    *   Strong to get rid of cooking smells.
    *   Usually go with a stove hood.
*   Shop Fans:

    *   Big and strong.
    *   For factories, warehouses, and workshops.
    *   Can handle dust, smoke, and heat.
*   Attic Fans:

    *   Go in attics.
    *   Cool down the attic and saves on AC costs.

4.  How They Work

The motor spins the fan blades. The blades pull air from the room and shoot it outside, sometimes through a pipe. Some fans have filters to catch dust or grease, mostly in kitchens.

5.  Parts of a Fan

*   Motor: Runs the fan.
*   Blades: The part that moves air. Made of plastic, aluminum, or steel.
*   Grill: Keeps stuff from hitting the blades and controls the airflow.
*   Body: Holds the motor and blades together.
*   Pipe (optional): Sends air outside.
*   Cap (optional): Stops air from coming back in.

6.  What to Look For

*   Airflow (CFM): How much air the fan moves.
*   Noise (dB): How loud the fan is.
*   Power: How much energy the fan uses.
*   Speed: One speed or multiple speeds.
*   Size: How big the fan is.

7.  Putting it In

*   Where: Put it near where the heat, smoke, or moisture is coming from.
*   How High: Higher up works better for hot air.
*   Pipes: Point the pipes straight outside; don't bend them too much.
*   Wiring: make sure the wires and fuses are good.
*   Seal it: Seal around the fan so air doesn't leak.

8.  Keeping it Running

*   Clean it: Dust and grease can slow it down.
*   Check the Motor: Oil it if you need to, or replace it if it's dead.
*   Check the Pipes: Make sure air can flow.
*   Change the Filter: If it has one, change it every few months.

9.  Problems and Fixes

| Problem          | Cause                         | Fix                           |
| :--------------- | :---------------------------- | :---------------------------- |
| Fan won't run    | No power, bad motor           | Check wires, replace motor    |
| Not enough air   | Blocked pipe, dirty blades   | Clean blades, clear the pipe |
| Loud             | Loose blades, bad motor       | Tighten blades, replace motor |
| Shaking          | Blades not balanced          | Fix or replace the blades     |
| Still smells/smoke | Fan isn't strong enough       | Get a stronger fan          |

10. Picking a Fan

*   Room Size: Get the right size fan for the room.
*   How Loud: Get a quiet fan for bedrooms/bathrooms.
*   Energy Use: Find a fan that doesn't use much power.
*   Toughness: Get one that can handle moisture or grease.
*   Pipes: Check if you need pipes to install it.
